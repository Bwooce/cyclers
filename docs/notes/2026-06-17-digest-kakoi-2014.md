## What the paper actually is

A trajectory-design study for departing **Earth-Moon L1 or L2 halo orbits
(EML1/L2)** to reach Mars, comparing four scenarios:

1. **Sun-Earth manifold transfer** (EML2 halo → SE L1/L2 halo via
   maneuver-free linked manifold → Mars via SE stable manifold + Hohmann)
2. **Earth-Moon manifold transfer** (EML2 halo → EM manifold → Earth
   perigee gravity assist → Mars)
3. **Direct transfer** (EM halo → Δv to leave halo → Earth flyby → Mars)
4. **Transfer with lunar flyby** (EML2 halo → EM manifold → lunar flyby
   → Earth flyby → Mars)

Method:

- **CR3BP blending** between Sun-Earth and Earth-Moon systems (Howell &
  Kakoi 2006); 3D orientation via Euler 3-1-3 angle sequence (α, i, β).
- **Multiple shooting** with free-variable/constraint formulation
  (Pavlak 2013).
- **Maneuver-free SE↔EM transfers** computed via phase-plot analysis on a
  hyperplane (ψ angle in SE frame).
- **Transition to ephemeris** with Sun, Earth, Moon, Mars positions.
- Mars ephemeris: incorporated either as gravitational force or as state
  vector only.

Quantitative results (selected tables):

**Table 2 (SE manifold transfer, EMAz=25,000 km, SEAz=163,200 km):**
- 1-maneuver: 2022-06-16 dep, 277 d TOF, Δv = 3.869 km/s
- 2-maneuver: 2022-06-16 dep, 350 d TOF, Δv = 3.495 km/s

**Table 3 (EM manifold L2 / L1 exterior, EMAz=25,000 km):**
- EML2 May 2026: 424 d, Δv = 0.921 km/s
- EML2 Jul 2028: 380 d, Δv = 0.759 km/s
- EML2 Oct 2030: 467 d, Δv = 1.245 km/s
- EML1 exterior May 2026: 441 d, Δv = 0.897 km/s
- EML1 exterior Jun 2028: 388 d, Δv = 0.802 km/s
- EML1 exterior Sep 2030: 487 d, Δv = **0.690 km/s** (best in table)

**Table 4 (EM L1 interior, EMAz=25,000 km):** 1.201-1.520 km/s.

**Table 5 (Direct transfers with lunar flyby, EMAz=25,000 km):**
- EML2 dep 2026/2028/2031: 272-343 d, Δv = 1.45-1.635 km/s
- EML1 dep 2026/2028/2031: 193-277 d, Δv = 1.215-1.575 km/s

**Table 6 (EML2 + lunar flyby, EMAz=5000 km):** 0.923-1.243 km/s, 218-292 d.

**Table 7 (L1/L2 direct + lunar flyby + Earth flyby, EMAz=25,000 km):**
1.082-1.842 km/s, 202-284 d.

**Table 8 (blended-model vs ephemeris comparison):** Δv values within a
few hundred m/s between models; departure dates from blended model are
reasonable initial guesses for ephemeris.

**Summary:** EM-manifold transfer with subsequent Earth gravity assist
gives the **lowest Δv** (~0.7-0.9 km/s for 2026-2030 opportunities) but
**longest TOF** (388-487 d). Direct transfers are faster (193-343 d) but
require ~0.6-0.9 km/s more.

## Catalogue / KNOWN_CORPUS relevance

**NOT a cycler paper.** This is a *one-way* Earth-Moon-libration-to-Mars
trajectory paper. The spacecraft departs EML1 or EML2, transits, arrives
at Mars — no repeated encounter, no resonance, no Tisserand-pump tour.

**Adjacent to #316 (cross-system cycler framework, completed)**: #316 is
about Sun-Earth ↔ Earth-Moon manifold-transit dynamics for cycler
construction. Kakoi 2014 is in the same dynamical regime (blended CR3BP
+ multiple shooting + manifold linkages) but applied to the *one-way
Mars-access* problem, not cycler construction. The methodology is
directly applicable — Kakoi 2014 is a sourced *prior art* reference for
any future Earth-Moon-libration-based cycler-onramp work.

### KNOWN_CORPUS impact

**No new anchor needed for a cycler.** Kakoi 2014 does not propose a

The current KNOWN_CORPUS does not have an Earth-Moon-libration → Mars
anchor; Kakoi 2014 would naturally pair with an existing or future

### Catalogue impact

**No new row.** The transfer is a *single-arc one-way transfer*, not a
cycler / quasi_cycler / mga_tour. It could be cited as the
*departure-side* (precursor) part of any future Earth-Moon-libration →

Reproducible discriminators for any future precursor row would be the
Table 3 EML1-exterior best Δv = 0.690 km/s (2030 dep), 487 d TOF — but
this only gets the spacecraft to Mars vicinity, not onto a cycler.
