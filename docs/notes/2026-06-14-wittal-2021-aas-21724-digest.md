## 1. State vectors / ICs / (Jacobi C, period T) / x-axis-crossing conditions? — NONE for any cycler

The paper has exactly three tables and a few equations. None is a cycler IC:

- **Table 1 (p. 3):** a qualitative complexity-scoring rubric (Comms / Interfaces /
  GN&C / Vehicle / Risk vs. a 0/5/10/15/20 scale). No numbers tied to any orbit.
- **Table 2 (p. 4):** CLPS commercial-provider cost-per-kg estimates (Intuitive
  Machines, Astrobotic, Masten, Firefly — contract dates, landed payload mass in kg,
  contract $M, $/kg). No trajectory data.
- **Table 3 (p. 5):** mission-concept comparison — Total Delta-V (km/s), Vehicle (t),
  Payload (t), Time (days), Complexity, Total Cost — for nine *payload-return* concepts
  (Lunar Direct CLPS/Standalone/Starship; BRT CLPS/Ridealong/Freeflyer/Starship;
  Gateway Direct; Starship Direct South Pole). These are mission-budget summaries, not
  orbit ICs. E.g. "BRT - Ridealong 0.15 km/s, 180 days"; "Gateway Direct 0.15 km/s,
  8 days." No state vectors, epochs, Jacobi constants, periods, or crossing conditions.
- **Equations 1-3:** the terminal cost function J = (1/2) pᵀ Q p, the parameter vector
  p = [dV, m_v, m_p, t, ψ], and the diagonal weight matrix Q. Pure mission-cost
  optimization algebra — nothing dynamical.

Quotable scalars that ARE present (all payload-return, none a cycler IC):
- BRT departs the Gateway NRHO; "the propulsive cost of returning that payload to an
  Earth Interface Point (EIP) can be as low as 20-30 m/s" (Sec. 3, p. 4-5).
- "Many small (< 10 m/s) midcourse corrections are likely to be required" on a BRT
  (Sec. 2.1, p. 3).
- BRT families "separated by one orbital period of the NRHO"; Fig. 2 caption: families
  shown "in the Sun-Earth rotating frame ... one period of ~6.5 days may still result
  in a very similar ballistic arc" (p. 3). [The ~6.5 d here is the NRHO period spacing
  between BRT family members, NOT a cycler period.]

**Conclusion for digest item 1:** No state vectors, no ICs, no (C, T) tuples, no x-axis
crossing conditions — for a cycler or for anything else. The trajectories appear only as
illustrative figures (Fig. 1 "not to scale," Fig. 2 BRT families) and as Table-3 budget
rows. Item-1 answer: **NEGATIVE, total.**

## 2. Dynamical model stated? — YES, and it is full N-body ephemeris + SRP (NOT CR3BP)

This is the one genuinely useful nugget, and it is *adjacent* corroboration rather than
a cycler-row unblock. The paper states its force model explicitly (Sec. 1.1
Assumptions, p. 2):

> "The gravitational model used includes the Earth, Moon, Sun, Venus, Jupiter, and
> Saturn for completeness, and assumes various cross-sectional areas and solar radiation
> pressures as is appropriate for each vehicle."

And the NRHO reference (Sec. 1, p. 2):

> "The Gateway Destination Orbit Model [8] is used as a baseline for Near-Rectilinear
> Halo Orbit (NRHO) arrival and departure ... " (ref [8] = D. Lee, "Gateway
> Destination Orbit Model: A Continuous 15 Year NRHO Reference Trajectory," NASA JSC
> White Paper, 2019 — a high-fidelity ephemeris reference).

So the Wittal group's modeling environment in this 2021 companion is a **high-fidelity,
multi-body (Earth + Moon + Sun + Venus + Jupiter + Saturn) ephemeris force model with
SRP** — emphatically NOT CR3BP, and richer than a bicircular model. This is consistent
with, and independently corroborates, the #211/#216 reclassification of the catalogue
row's `model_assumption` from `cr3bp` to `analytic-ephemeris` (which was inferred from
IAC-22 internal evidence: approximate ~27±3 d period, per-flyby dV accumulation,
cycle-to-cycle inclination variation). It does NOT prove the IAC-22 cyclers used this
exact stack (different paper, different trajectory class), but it shows the same authors'
house tooling is ephemeris-class N-body, strengthening the inference.
