## What the paper actually is

A comprehensive undergraduate-to-graduate astrodynamics textbook. The
9-chapter body covers:

- **Ch. 1 Fundamentals** (mathematical models, coordinate systems, vector
  algebra) — pp. 1-22
- **Ch. 2 Keplerian Motion** (two-body, vis-viva, geometric formulas) —
  pp. 23-60
- **Ch. 3 Orbital Maneuvers** (TCMs, Hohmann, bi-elliptic, **§3.5
  Interplanetary Trajectories: hyperbolic, gravity assist, patched conics,
  types & examples, target space, targeting & insertion**) — pp. 61-138
- **Ch. 4 Techniques of Astrodynamics** (Battin universal formulas,
  Keplerian elements, **§4.4 Lambert's Problem, §4.5 celestial mechanics
  incl. n-body, SoI**) — pp. 139-225
- **Ch. 5 Non-Keplerian Motion** (perturbations, J2, satellite paradox,
  Zero G) — pp. 227-249
- **Ch. 6 Spacecraft Rendezvous** (Hill-Clohessy-Wiltshire, Apollo 11
  rendezvous example, Mars Sample Return) — pp. 251-269
- **Ch. 7 Navigation & Mission Design Tools** (FPVS, maneuver design,
  Free-Return Circumlunar Methods 1 & 2, Apollo 11 / Apollo 13 return) —
  pp. 271-347
- **Ch. 8 Mission Design → Flight Operations** (Galileo precision maneuver
  parameters, MATLAB ODE, collision avoidance, EOM) — pp. 349-367
- **Ch. 9 Further Study** (compact 1-3 page summaries with references for
  20 topics: launch, EDL, aerogravity assist, OD, OpNav, autonomous nav,
  attitude, constellations, formation flying, CR3BP, RTBP, Lagrange &
  IPS, 4-body design, solar sailing, **§9.2.18 CYCLERS**, propulsion) —
  pp. 369-385
- **Appendices A-D** (vector analysis review; student projects; Penzo
  parametric plots; **Mars opportunities 2024-2033 porkchop plots**) —
  pp. 387-427
- **References** (188 numbered entries) — pp. 432-439
- **Index** — pp. 441-461

The book is heavily anchored in Hintz's 37-year JPL operational experience
(Viking, Mariner 9, Voyager II, Pioneer Venus Orbiter, Galileo, Ulysses,
Cassini-Huygens, Aquarius), so it has authoritative weight on
mission-operations methodology — but it is a **textbook**, not a research
contribution. The cycler-specific content is a 1-page survey.

## Deep-read: §9.2.18 Cyclers (page 383)

The only cycler-specific section. Key content (page 383):

- **Definition (verbatim):** "A spacecraft on a cycler regularly passes
  close to both Earth and Mars (but never stops at either). The
  'passenger' vehicle enters or leaves the cycler at the appropriate
  planet."
- **Classification verbatim:** "Cyclers that require propulsive maneuvers
  are referred to as powered cyclers, whereas cyclers that rely only on
  gravitational forces are referred to as ballistic cyclers. There are
  some variations on cyclers, in which the spacecraft enters a temporary
  parking orbit at Mars (semi-cyclers), at Earth (reverse semi-cyclers),
  or at both Earth and Mars (stop-over cyclers)."
- **Reference 2 framing:** "Reference 2 analyzes all cyclers that repeat
  every two synodic periods and have one intermediate Earth encounter.
  In the reference, the Earth-Mars synodic period is assumed, at least
  initially, to be **2 1/7 years**."
- **Aldrin Mars Cycler:** quoted from Buzz Aldrin's website —
  "five-and-a-half-month journey from the Earth to Mars, and a return
  trip to Earth of about the same duration on a twin semi-cycler."
- **Cited references:**
  1. Buzz Aldrin's website (buzzaldrin.com/aldrin-mars-cycler/)
  2. **McConaghy, Yam, Landau, Longuski**, "Two-Synodic-Period Earth-Mars
     Cyclers With Intermediate Earth Encounter," AAS 03-509, AAS/AIAA
     Astrodynamics Specialists Conference, Big Sky, Montana, Aug 3-7,
     2003.
  3. **Chen, McConaghy, Landau, Longuski**, "A Powered Earth-Mars Cycler
     with Three Synodic-Period Repeat Time," AAS 03-510, same conference.

## Other cycler-adjacent sections (chapter-summary depth)

- **§9.2.13 CR3BP (p. 377):** standard textbook CR3BP definition
  (negligible 3rd-mass, point-mass spherically-symmetric primaries on
  circular orbits about barycenter). References: Curtis 2019, Pollard
  1966, Villac/Chow/Lo/Hintz 2011. NO cycler content.
- **§9.2.14 RTBP (p. 379):** 3 scalar equations of motion (incl. Coriolis
  terms) — equilibrium / Lagrange-point analysis only. NO cycler content.
- **§9.2.15 Lagrange Points & Interplanetary Superhighway (p. 380):**
  qualitative L1-L5 description; JWST at L2; cites Gomez-Jorba-Simo-
  Masdemont *Dynamics & Mission Design Near Libration Points* (Vols I-IV,
  World Scientific 2001), Koon-Lo-Marsden-Ross "Heteroclinic Orbits
  between Periodic Orbits and Resonance Transitions" *Chaos* 10(2) 2000,
  Koon-Lo-Marsden-Ross "Shoot the Moon" AAS 00-166 (2000), "Constructing
  a Low Energy Transfer Between Jovian Moons" *Contemporary Math* 292
  (2002), Lo & Ross "Lunar L1 Gateway: Portal to the Stars and Beyond"
  AIAA Space 2001, Lo "Interplanetary Superhighway and Origins Program"
  IEEE 2002. **All these are foundational manifold-dynamics references
  already known to the literature; not new corpus material.**
- **§9.2.16 Four-Body Trajectory Design (p. 381):** Hiten / Lunar A /
  Genesis return — cites Miller AAS 03-144 only. Reference-only.
- **§3.5.4 Types and Examples of Interplanetary Missions (pp. 105-113):**
  patched-conic, V_∞ vector, B-plane, gravity-assist; standard graduate
  methodology. No cycler-specific content.
- **§4.4 Lambert's Problem (pp. 163-184):** standard Battin universal
  formulas. No cycler-specific content.
- **Appendix D Mars opportunities 2024-2033 (pp. 419-427):** porkchop
  contour plots (C3 / TFL / VHA / DAL) for Earth-Mars and Mars-Earth in
  2024, 2026, 2028, 2031, 2033. References: Knocke et al. JPL D-7278
  (1990) for 2024-2026 and **Burke-Falck-McGuire NASA/TM-2010-216764
  "Interplanetary Mission Design Handbook Earth-Mars Mission
  Opportunities 2026-2045"** for 2026-2033. These are one-way Lambert
  porkchops, not cyclers.
