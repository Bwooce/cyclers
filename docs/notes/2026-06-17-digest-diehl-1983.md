## 2. What the paper actually is

A **JPL Mission Design Office Jovian satellite tour design paper** for the pre-launch Galileo concept — written when launch was projected for "late May 1986" via Space Shuttle + Centaur F upper stage on a **DIRECT E→J trajectory** (not VEEGA). Per the abstract (p.1):

> "In 1986, the United States will launch Galileo, the first spacecraft to Jupiter to deliver an atmospheric probe and to perform a tour of the Galilean satellites. The gravity assist resulting from one satellite encounter will target the Orbiter to the next satellite encounter. In this manner, repeated close encounters with Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto are used to shape and control the Orbiter trajectory."

The interplanetary trajectory shown in Fig 1 (p.1) is a DIRECT Earth-launch May 1986 → ΔV January 1987 (post-apoapsis maneuver to change heliocentric inclination) → **Jupiter arrival August 1988**. This is the pre-Challenger, pre-VEEGA design — Galileo was originally going to fly direct to Jupiter using STS+Centaur, with launch period beginning late May 1986. The Challenger disaster (January 1986) cancelled Centaur-G aboard Shuttle and forced the IUS-VEEGA redesign that ultimately flew in 1989.

The paper's scope is **the Jovian satellite tour itself** — the gravity-assist sequence among Io/Europa/Ganymede/Callisto after JOI. The interplanetary heliocentric leg is explicitly deferred to a companion paper:

> "A detailed discussion of the interplanetary trajectory design for the Galileo mission is provided by D'Amario and Byrnes.¹" (p.1)

where Ref 1 (p.11) = "D'Amario, L.A., and D.V. Byrnes, 'Interplanetary Trajectory Design for the Galileo Mission,' Paper AIAA-83-0099, presented at AIAA 21st Aerospace Sciences Meeting, Reno, Nevada, Jan. 1983."

Seven sections plus references:

1. **Introduction** (p.1)
2. **Gravity Assist Trajectory Design** (pp.2–3): bend-angle physics and prior-art summary (Niehoff 1971, Minovitch 1972, Beckman-Smith 1973, Uphoff-Roberts-Friedman 1974, Diehl-Nock 1979)
3. **Mission Constraints and Science Requirements** (pp.3–5): radiation dose ≤150 krad, ΔV budget 205 m/s (deterministic + statistical 90% probability), Table 1 (science requirements), Table 2 (terminology), Table 3 (Galilean satellite physical data)
4. **Tour Design Strategies** (pp.5–7): equatorial orbit, satellite coverage, nontargeted encounters, tail-petal magnetotail orbit, Jupiter occultations, perijove raise
5. **Strawman Satellite Tour** (pp.7–9): the 11-encounter representative tour, Table 4 (dated encounter sequence), Table 5 (per-orbit radiation dosage), Fig 4 (tour petal plot)
6. **Tour Design Tools** (pp.9–10): JOPROBE (probe/orbit insertion sim), STOUR (conic point-to-point tour designer), MOSES (Multiple Orbit Satellite Encounter Software multiconic optimization), DTOUR (nontargeted-encounter screening). Table 6 = STOUR-vs-MOSES aimpoint comparison
7. **Summary** (p.11)

## 6. Errata vs prior assumptions

1. **"Diehl-Belbruno-Roberts" authorship is wrong** — definitively. Real authors: Diehl-Kaplan-Penzo. **#354 D'Amario 1992 digest (§2) flagged this as suspect**; this paper confirms.
2. **"1986" year is wrong** — the actual paper is 1983. The "1986" was the planned launch date in the abstract.
3. **"Galileo VEEGA" scope is wrong** — this paper does not discuss VEEGA at all. It treats a DIRECT E→J concept (Fig 1 shows the simple Hohmann-like transfer with a single mid-cruise ΔV). VEEGA was a post-Challenger redesign.
4. **Companion paper AIAA-83-0099** (D'Amario-Byrnes 1983) is the interplanetary leg; it is referenced (Ref 1, p.1) but not in the private paper corpus. Acquiring it would complete the pre-Challenger 1983 Galileo design record.
5. **D'Amario-Byrnes-Stanford 1981** (J. Guidance & Control 4(6), Nov-Dec 1981) is the canonical MOSES multiconic optimization paper (Ref 13, p.12). Not in the private paper corpus.
6. **Diehl-Nock 1979** (AAS Paper 79-141 Provincetown) is the original "Galileo Jupiter Encounter and Satellite Tour Trajectory Design" reference (Ref 7, p.11) — Diehl's earlier solo collaboration. Predates the Aug 1980 Nock "Interplanetary Trajectory Options for Project Galileo" (AIAA Paper 80-1697, Ref 8).
