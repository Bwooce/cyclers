## 2. What the paper actually is

A **tutorial-and-review** exposition (the section heading "Tutorials and Reviews" appears on the top-right banner of p.1353) explaining how the two-point boundary-value-problem continuation software **AUTO** [Doedel 1981; Doedel et al. 1997; Doedel et al. 2000] can be applied to conservative dynamical systems that lack an explicit continuation parameter. The technical novelty is the **unfolding-term regularization** (Sec. 2.3-2.4 + 4.1): adding an unfolding parameter `λ` multiplied by the gradient of the conserved quantity (or quantities, plural for multi-integral systems), which restores genericity to the BVP without distorting the periodic solutions (λ vanishes identically on the solution branch — this is the content of Theorem 2.1, p.1358).

The paper has three primary computational demonstrations:
1. **A 2D model conservative system** (Sec. 2.3) with one first integral, illustrating that the introduction of λ x₂ as an unfolding term collapses the otherwise-vertical bifurcation diagram into a one-dimensional family computable by standard pseudo-arclength continuation (Eq. 7, Fig. 2).
2. **A guided tour of the Earth-Moon CR3BP (µ = 0.01215)** periodic-orbit landscape (Sec. 3), tracing the Lyapunov-Vertical-Halo-Connecting-Magenta family network via a sequence of pitchfork / transcritical bifurcations starting at L1, terminating in the L4/L5 Vertical families. The schematic in Fig. 4 and the seven subsequent 3D rendering figures (Figs. 5-14) are the visual product.
3. **A continuation of the Chenciner-Montgomery figure-8 orbit** through the planar general 3-body problem (Sec. 4), as the mass m₁ of one body is varied. Sec. 4.2 documents a *local* continuation that detects a fold (LP) and a pitchfork (BP) within a 1e-5 neighborhood of m₁ = 1 (Fig. 16(c)). Sec. 4.3 documents the *global* continuation reducing m₁ all the way to 0, where the orbit becomes a periodic solution of the restricted 3-body problem with µ = 0.5 (Solution 7, Fig. 19f) — **establishing a continuous numerical homotopy from the figure-8 to a CR3BP periodic orbit**. The homotopy "requires branch switching at the bifurcation point denoted as Solution 2" (p.1376).

The paper is a *methodology* paper, not a discovery paper: every CR3BP family it shows (Lyapunov, Vertical, Halo, the Y/C/M connecting families) is previously known, and the family-network connectivity it documents was assembled from the prior numerical literature it cites (Hadjidemetriou 1975, Ichtiaroglou-Michalodimitrakis 1980, Markellos 1981, Howell 1984, Howell-Campbell 1999, Hénon 1997/2001, Zagouras 1985, Papadakis-Zagouras 1992). The novelty is *that AUTO can trace all of it as a single computational system from L1 outward, with bifurcations detected and branch-switched automatically.*

A precise scope caveat (paper's own, footnote 1 p.1359): "**In this paper we reserve the term *bifurcation point* (or *bifurcation orbit*), when not further qualified, for transcritical and pitchfork bifurcations, excluding period-doubling, torus and subharmonic bifurcations.**" Period-doubling bifurcations ARE mentioned (B2 in Fig. 7 is a "reverse period-doubling"), but the term "bifurcation point" without qualifier means a non-trivial Floquet multiplier reaching +1 — which is exactly the **saddle-center / pitchfork / transcritical** class #347 Phase 1 needs.

## 3. Bifurcation detection algorithm

### 3.1 The detection mechanism (AUTO test functions)

The paper does not give a free-standing test-function formula in the main text. The detection mechanism is described twice in shared form:

- **p.1355, §2.2** (verbatim, last paragraph): "*AUTO determines the characteristic multipliers (or Floquet multipliers), that reflect asymptotic stability and bifurcation properties, as a by-product of the decomposition of the Jacobian of the boundary value collocation system* [Doedel et al., 1991c; Fairgrieve & Jepson, 1991; see also Lust, 2001]."
- **p.1359, §3.1** (verbatim, third paragraph, after eq. 16): "*However, as orbits in six-dimensional phase space, i.e. as solutions of Eq. (14), there are various bifurcations from the Lyapunov orbits. On portions of the solution branch the orbits have complex multipliers that move around the unit circle in the complex plane, as can be determined numerically using AUTO, giving rise to invariant tori, subharmonic bifurcations and period-doubling bifurcations. There are also bifurcation points, where distinct solution branches intersect, with identical orbit and period at the point of intersection.*"

The algorithm itself is therefore: **Floquet multipliers are recomputed at each continuation step from the BVP collocation Jacobian decomposition, then watched as scalar test functions** (modulus and argument as functions of arclength). At a bifurcation, a non-trivial multiplier crosses +1; AUTO's branch-point detector flags the sign change of the relevant test function and brackets the bifurcation by secant interpolation. This is the standard Doedel et al. 1991b,c routine — pp.493-520 and 745-772 in IJBC vol. 1 (cited p.1377). The 2003 paper does NOT redefine it.

### 3.2 The Floquet multiplier structure for conservative systems

The paper formalizes the trivial-multiplier structure carefully:

- **CR3BP (autonomous Hamiltonian with one integral, eq. 14)**: Lyapunov orbits as 4D-phase-space solutions (eq. 16) have "two Floquet multipliers equal to 1, one real multiplier greater than one, and one real multiplier less than one" (p.1359, §3.1, ¶3). The pair (+1, +1) is the *energy / time-translation* trivial pair — algebraic multiplicity 2, geometric multiplicity 1 (Theorem 2.1, p.1358). The other real reciprocal pair is the stable/unstable manifold direction.
- **Full 3D CR3BP (six-dim. state, eq. 14)**: same structure, plus a pair on the unit circle (Lyapunov orbits acquire a center direction in 3D).
- **General 3-body problem (Hamiltonian + 3 linear momenta + 3 angular momenta = 7 integrals, eq. 17 → eq. 18)**: 18 Floquet multipliers, in reciprocal pairs (ψ, 1/ψ). **At least 14 of the 18 are forced to +1 if angular momentum L = 0; at least 12 if L ≠ 0** (p.1369, ¶2). For the figure-8 (L=0), 14 trivial + 4 nontrivial. The 4 nontrivial are ψ_j = exp(±2πiν_j), ν₁ = 0.008422, ν₂ = 0.298092 (p.1369, ¶2; agrees with Simó).

The paper's *justifying* theorem for unfolding-based continuation is **Theorem 2.1 (p.1358)**: *"Let x₀ be a solution of Eq. (10) whose monodromy matrix V(1) has 1 as an eigenvalue with geometric multiplicity 1. Then there exists a unique branch of solutions of F(p,T,λ)=0 passing through (x₀(0), T₀, 0). Moreover the parameter λ vanishes along this branch."*

This is the **regular-point continuation theorem** for conservative systems: geometric-multiplicity-1 at the trivial +1 ensures the BVP Jacobian (augmented with the unfolding parameter as a free variable and the phase condition as a closing constraint) is full rank, and the Implicit Function Theorem delivers a smooth one-parameter family. **A bifurcation point is precisely where this hypothesis FAILS** — geometric multiplicity ≥ 2 at the +1 eigenvalue, equivalently a second non-trivial Floquet multiplier arriving at +1.

### 3.3 Saddle-center / fold detection (the LP test)

The paper labels folds **"LP" (limit point)** and detects them as **sign-changes of the determinant of the BVP collocation Jacobian** (standard AUTO behavior, not redefined here). The most explicit place this appears in the paper:

- **Fig. 16(c), p.1372**: a fold (LP) and a pitchfork (BP) are both flagged on the figure-8 primary branch within m₁ ∈ [0.99980, 1.00005]. The (L₂-norm, m₁) plot shows BP on the left and LP on the right, with the bifurcating pitchfork branch shown as dashed and the primary as solid.
- **p.1374, ¶2** (verbatim): "*Examining the nonzero values of the logarithm of the modulus, one can identify two bifurcations in this diagram, namely, the fold bifurcation mentioned above, and a pitchfork bifurcation at a value of m₁ just below 1.*"
- **Fig. 18, p.1374**: log|µᵢ| and Arg(µᵢ) for the complex-conjugate Floquet pair closest to +1, as a function of m₁. The pair sits exactly on the unit circle with non-zero argument in the elliptic region near the figure-8 (m₁ ≈ 0.9999-1.0000), then **the imaginary part collapses to zero, the pair coalesces on the real axis at +1, and the multipliers split into two reals**. The point where Arg(µ) crosses zero is the bifurcation (LP or BP depending on the further direction). **This is the visual saddle-center signature** — the elliptic pair colliding at +1 — exactly the mechanism #347 Phase 1 P1.1 noted as the (3,2) anchor's signature (complex pair 0.6448 ± 0.7643j on the unit circle, awaiting collision at +1).

### 3.4 Pitchfork detection (the BP test) and the symmetry distinction

A pitchfork bifurcation differs from a fold in that **a second branch genuinely emerges**, transverse to the primary, with the bifurcating branch BREAKING a symmetry of the primary. In AUTO's continuation framework these are both "bifurcation points" (footnote 1 definition), detected by the same Floquet-+1 test function but distinguished by **branching dimension** (codim 1 with one zero singular value = fold; codim 1 with one zero singular value plus a non-zero second null vector = pitchfork). The standard AUTO routine is described in Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b,c (cited p.1377-1378).

**The key fact for #347** is that the paper does NOT separate saddle-center from pitchfork by the multiplier signature — both have a non-trivial multiplier at +1. Distinction is by the **branching pattern**: fold = the branch turns back in the continuation parameter; pitchfork = a transverse branch emerges. Both are flagged by the same test function. This matches RTR2026's statement that the saddle-center generates **two** branches (stable + unstable) at the maximum of C (which is a fold in the (C, x₀) plane).

### 3.5 Period-doubling, torus, subharmonic

Mentioned but not the primary focus. The footnote 1 (p.1359) explicitly excludes these from the paper's use of "bifurcation point". B2 in Fig. 7 (p.1363) is labelled a "reverse period-doubling" — detected by a non-trivial multiplier reaching −1 — and connects the Vertical orbits to a family of circular planar orbits. The Halo family Fig. 9-10 has a period-doubling-type planar bifurcation as well. AUTO's detector handles all of these by watching the multipliers; the difference is the target value on the unit circle (+1 for fold/pitchfork, −1 for period-doubling, exp(2πik/n) for k:n resonance / subharmonic, off-axis on unit circle for Neimark-Sacker / torus).

## 4. Branch-switching corrector

### 4.1 The branch-switching primitive

The paper's branch-switching algorithm is not given as a formula in the main text — it is invoked by name (**"branch switching"**, p.1376 ¶4) and cited to the underlying AUTO library (Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b,c). The most explicit operational statement appears in §4.3 (verbatim, p.1376): "*Our computations therefore establish a homotopy from the figure-8 orbit of Chenciner and Montgomery to a periodic solution of the restricted 3-body problem. **The complete homotopy path requires branch switching at the bifurcation point denoted as Solution 2.** More extensive computations, that we do not report here, establish the existence of further homotopy paths from the figure-8 orbit to other solutions of the restricted 3-body problem. All these paths pass through Solution 2 and switch to the branch of 3D orbits there.*"

The algorithm is therefore: **at the flagged bifurcation point**, AUTO **computes the null space of the rank-deficient BVP Jacobian** (the standard null-vector / kernel computation), **identifies the bifurcating branch direction as the second null vector** (the first is the tangent to the primary), **and seeds Newton with the primary IC perturbed by an ε-multiple of this second null vector** — handing off to the BVP corrector to converge on the bifurcating branch. This is the Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b,c recipe.

### 4.2 Where this is operationalized in the paper

Three concrete branch-switching events documented:

1. **Lyapunov → Halo** (Sec. 3.2, Fig. 5 / Fig. 9-10, p.1361-1364): a bifurcation point on the planar Lyapunov family gives rise to the 3D Halo family. The bifurcating direction is the out-of-plane component (z) — the branch breaks the z=ż=0 invariance of the planar manifold.
2. **Halo → cyan family** (Sec. 3.2, Fig. 12, p.1365): two symmetry-related pitchfork bifurcations on the Halo branch generate the connecting cyan loop.
3. **Figure-8 → Solution 2 → 3D nonplanar branch → ... → Solution 7 = CR3BP orbit** (Sec. 4.3, Fig. 16(a), p.1372 + Fig. 19, p.1375): the figure-8 family hits a pitchfork at Solution 2, the bifurcating branch consists of non-planar orbits with non-zero angular momentum, and continuation along this branch (varying m₁) eventually reaches m₁=0 where Body 1 vanishes and the surviving 2-body problem (m₂ = m₃ = 1) with the test body is a CR3BP at µ=0.5. **This is the paper's headline result.**

### 4.3 ε-perturbation amplitude

The paper does NOT publish a specific ε value. AUTO's default (per Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b and standard AUTO config) is **ε = 0.01 × continuation arclength step**, with the corrector tolerance providing self-validation. The #347 Phase 1 design doc's P1.4 plan to sweep ε ∈ {1e-4, 1e-3, 1e-2} is consistent with AUTO practice.

### 4.4 What's NOT in the paper (honest negative)

- **No explicit formula** for the kernel computation at a rank-deficient BVP Jacobian. Cited to Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b,c (which are in IJBC vol. 1 and would need to be acquired).
- **No explicit handling of degenerate cases** (geometric multiplicity > 1 at +1, double-null kernel). The paper assumes the "generic" pitchfork — codim 1 with rank deficit 1.
- **No commentary on multi-shooting** for unstable bifurcation neighborhoods. AUTO is single-shooting via collocation; the project's `multi_shooting.py` would be a complementary upgrade if collocation-via-DOP853-shooting fails near the bifurcation point.

## 6. Comparison vs RTR2026

**The two papers describe the same canonical bifurcation mechanism** — a non-trivial Floquet multiplier reaching +1 — but **frame it differently** and **apply it to different objects**.

### Where they AGREE

- **Mechanism**: both identify a non-trivial Floquet multiplier crossing +1 as the bifurcation condition. RTR2026 names this "**saddle-center**" specifically for the cycler-family maximum-C limit (RTR2026 Fig. 4 p.5 + verbatim quote in Phase 0 doc §2). Doedel 2003 covers both saddle-center (fold/LP, p.1372) and pitchfork (BP, p.1372, 1374) — same multiplier signature, distinguished by branching pattern.
- **Stability flip**: both note that at the bifurcation, stability character changes — "*at the fold the solution loses stability and becomes hyperbolic*" (Doedel 2003 p.1371 §4.2 ¶7) vs RTR2026's "*two branches of periodic orbits near the bifurcation point — a stable branch and an unstable branch*".
- **Branch creation in pairs**: explicitly stated in RTR2026 (two branches); implicit in Doedel via the fold/pitchfork structure (a fold's two branches are the same family folding back; a pitchfork has a primary plus a transverse symmetry-breaking branch).

### Where they DIFFER

| Aspect | Doedel 2003 | RTR2026 |
|---|---|---|
| **Continuation primitive** | Pseudo-arclength on a BVP collocation system with unfolding-parameter regularization (Eq. 5b, p.1355) | Natural-parameter (vary C hold x₀, vary x₀ hold C) on a shooting system with symmetric Poincaré-half-period closure (Phase 0 §2) |
| **Where folds fit** | Pseudo-arclength passes through folds without stopping; the LP is detected post-hoc by sign-change of test function | Natural-parameter FAILS at folds (continuation parameter exhausts); this is the project's #347 P1.2 risk |
| **Branch-switching primitive** | AUTO's kernel-computation at rank-deficient BVP Jacobian, seed Newton with primary IC + ε·(second null vector) | Project-internal `family_switch.py` does eigenvector perturbation in 6D state, but always re-corrects via symmetric corrector → symmetric basin collapse (the #284/#343 root cause) |
| **What's "bifurcation"** | Footnote 1 p.1359: pitchfork + transcritical only (the +1 family) | Saddle-center as a class (covers fold and pitchfork in the symmetric framework) |
| **Test object** | Libration-point families (Lyapunov, Vertical, Halo, …) + figure-8 | Earth-Moon prograde cyclers (1,1)a, (1,1)b, (2,1), (3,2) |
| **Symmetry handling** | Symmetries are broken automatically when the bifurcating branch emerges (Halo → "northern"/"southern" is the post-bifurcation symmetry pair) | Symmetric corrector is the *only* corrector exercised; asymmetric branch is the project's open frontier |
| **Sourced numerics** | Pitchfork m₁ value (p.1374): "just below 1" (visible in Fig. 16(c) at m₁ ≈ 0.99989); fold m₁ ≈ 1.00005; Floquet ν₁ = 0.008422, ν₂ = 0.298092 for figure-8 | C* for (3,2) cycler saddle-center not published (an open numerical question for #347 P1.2) |

### Are they complementary, redundant, or contradictory?

**Complementary.** RTR2026 supplies the *cycler-specific anchor* (a sourced symmetric (3,2) IC and the qualitative saddle-center claim). Doedel 2003 supplies the *general numerical methodology* (pseudo-arclength BVP, unfolding parameter, AUTO's bifurcation detector, AUTO's branch-switching primitive, plus a fully worked example of branch-switching through a pitchfork = §4.2-4.3 figure-8 → CR3BP). **Neither paper supplies an explicit branch-switching pseudocode** — Doedel 2003 cites it to Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b,c (IJBC vol. 1 pp. 493-520 and 745-772) which are NOT in the corpus.

There is no contradiction. The methodology Doedel 2003 describes WOULD discover RTR2026's saddle-center if run on the (3,2) symmetric cycler family with an appropriate BVP setup. **#347 Phase 1 is implementing a project-internal version of the Doedel methodology, anchored on RTR2026's sourced IC**, which is the correct synthesis.

## 7. Phase 1 implications

The in-flight #347 Phase 1 work (P1.1 PASSED at commit `4b840a3`, P1.2 in progress per `scripts/floquet_phase1_p1_2_walk.py`) is methodologically aligned with Doedel 2003. **If Phase 1 hits a wall, the canonical Doedel references are:**

### 7.1 Saddle-center / pitchfork detection (P1.3 fallback)

**If P1.3 fails to flag a single saddle-center on the (3,2) family within the P1.2 range:** the canonical reference is **Doedel 2003 §3.1 ¶3 (p.1359) + Fig. 18 (p.1374)**. The signature is:
- **Watch the complex-conjugate Floquet pair closest to +1** as a scalar test function (modulus and argument).
- **Saddle-center / pitchfork = the pair's argument crosses zero while modulus is ≈1** (the pair coalesces on the unit circle at +1, then splits into two reals).
- **Trivial pair exclusion**: the algebraic-multiplicity-2 unit pair (energy + time-translation, geometric multiplicity 1 per Theorem 2.1 p.1358) is *always* at +1 in a single-integral conservative system. The detector must distinguish "the always-+1 trivial pair" from "an additional non-trivial multiplier arriving at +1". The cleanest test: scan the eigenvalue closest to +1 *after excluding the two of smallest geometric distance from +1 from a baseline reference orbit*.

The #347 Phase 1 P1.1 progress note already nails the (3,2) anchor's structure: dominant unstable reciprocal pair (~2.5e5 and 3.5e-6), trivial pair near +1 (split 1.0147/0.9855 by integrator precision), and **the unit-circle complex pair 0.6448 ± 0.7643j** that will collide at +1 on its way to the saddle-center. Doedel Fig. 18 (p.1374) is the textbook picture of this collision.

### 7.2 Branch-switching corrector (P1.4 fallback)

**If P1.4's asymmetric corrector fails to converge from the perturbed seed:** the canonical reference is **Doedel 2003 p.1376 ¶4 + Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b,c (IJBC vol. 1)**. The recipe (synthesized from Doedel 2003 + standard AUTO practice):
1. **At the bracketed bifurcation point**, recompute the BVP Jacobian (the project's monodromy + STM substrate).
2. **Compute its null space**: the *first* null vector is the family tangent (the primary branch direction). The *second* null vector — orthogonal to the first — is the **bifurcating branch direction** in the (state ⊕ T ⊕ continuation-parameter) augmented space.
3. **Seed the corrector** with the bifurcation-point IC + ε × (second null vector). The ε should be small but not infinitesimal — AUTO's default is ε ≈ 0.01 × arclength step (no value published in the 2003 paper); the Phase 0 P1.4 sweep of {1e-4, 1e-3, 1e-2} is consistent.
4. **Use the asymmetric corrector** (full-period closure, not symmetric half-period). This is the critical distinction from `family_switch.py:switch_family` — which the Phase 0 design doc §2 already identified.

**Implementation gap Doedel 2003 cannot fill from the main text**: the explicit kernel-computation routine for a rank-deficient BVP Jacobian at the bifurcation point. The cited references Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b,c are NOT in the private paper corpus. If P1.4 needs deeper detail than the recipe above, acquiring **Doedel-Keller-Kernévez 1991b ("Numerical analysis and control of bifurcation problems: I", IJBC 1, 493-520)** would unblock — this is the canonical IJBC reference for the AUTO branch-switching algorithm. Action item §10.A.

### 7.3 Convergence near the bifurcation (P1.4 numerical risk)

The Phase 0 §5 risk note flagged: "*Does the project's DOP853 integrator hold corrector tolerance at the bifurcation? Near a saddle-center, the monodromy has multipliers near (+1, +1, +1, +1, …) — clustered eigenvalues are ill-conditioned.*" Doedel 2003 confirms the eigenvalue clustering risk implicitly (§4.2 p.1371: "The smallness of ν₁ indicates that the corresponding multipliers are very close to a double +1 situation" — for the figure-8, this is what enabled the fold/pitchfork detection to work numerically). The paper uses **orthogonal collocation with adaptive mesh selection** (p.1355 §2.2 ¶5; Russell & Christiansen 1978), which "*also has the desirable property of preserving the symplectic structure of Hamiltonian systems*". The project's DOP853 single-shooting is not symplectic; if Phase 1 sees integrator-precision-limited eigenvalue separation, the **mitigation is multi-shooting** (which Phase 0 P1.4 mentions) or switching to a symplectic integrator (Stormer-Verlet, Yoshida-4, …) just at the bifurcation neighborhood.

### 7.4 The unfolding-parameter trick (P1.x advanced fallback)

If the continuation in C alone (one parameter) becomes ill-conditioned, **Doedel 2003 §2.4 Eq. (10) (p.1357)** offers the unfolding trick: introduce λ as a free continuation parameter, attach λ × ∇C(x) to the right-hand side, and run pseudo-arclength on the augmented system. λ ≡ 0 on the actual periodic-solution branch (Theorem 2.1, p.1358); a non-zero λ during a continuation step means the corrector is off-manifold and indicates step adjustment is needed. **This is a more robust alternative to natural-parameter continuation in C alone** and should be considered if the Phase 1 P1.2 step-adaptation work runs into trouble.

## 8. Phase 2+ implications

**If #347 graduates to a discovery-sweep Phase 2 (multi-family Earth-Moon sweep per Phase 0 §6), Doedel 2003 adds three things over RTR2026:**

1. **Fig. 4-style network mapping is the natural data structure**. RTR2026 documents the (3,2) family in isolation (Fig. 4 of RTR2026 = a single (C, x₀) plot). Doedel 2003 shows that the *natural object* once you have a bifurcation framework is a **family network** (Fig. 4 of Doedel 2003 = a 3D rendering of all connected libration-point families).
