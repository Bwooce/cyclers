## 2. What the paper actually is

A **1964 NASA Lewis foundational paper** that frames and solves the **manned
nonstop Mars round-trip trajectory problem**: launch from Earth, single pass
near Mars (no Mars-orbit insertion), return to Earth — comparing three
distinct turn-mechanisms at Mars:

1. **Gravity turn** (Mars gravity only modifies the trajectory; arrival and
   departure V_inf magnitudes are equal)
2. **Propulsive-gravity turn** (small chemical Delta-V at Mars sphere of
   influence supplements the gravity turn)
3. **Aerodynamic turn** (vehicle dips into Mars atmosphere; lift and drag
   modify the velocity vector through angle phi_a)

Results compared for **1971 and 1980 launch years** on the metric pair
{total trip time, propulsive Delta-V}. The 1971 trips pass Mars near
perihelion (favourable); 1980 passes Mars near aphelion (unfavourable);
1971 trips need ~1 mps less Delta-V on the gravity and propulsive-gravity
schemes (Fig 3 vs Fig 4, p. 369).

This paper **predates Aldrin's 1985 cycler concept by 21 years** and
predates the McConaghy-Longuski 2004 nPr cycler nomenclature by 40 years.
It is the **earliest known sourced study of single-flyby Earth-Mars-Earth
ballistic free-return trajectories**.

## 3. Key content, tables, equations with page citations

### 3.1 The three turn mechanisms (Figs 1-2, p. 369)

- Fig 1 (p. 369): Heliocentric trajectory geometry. Vehicle departs Earth
  at point 1, passes Mars at point 2, returns to Earth at point 3.
  Outbound and inbound legs are of unequal length (this is the FUNDAMENTAL
  cycler-precursor insight, asymmetric ballistic E-M-E trajectory).
- Fig 2 (p. 369): Three turn types at Mars sphere of influence:
  - 2a: gravity turn (V_A = V_D in magnitude, phi_GA = phi_GD; gravity-
    only deflection through total angle 2*phi_G)
  - 2a (overlay): propulsive-gravity turn (impulse DeltaV_d applied at
    departure SOI to rotate V_D' from V_D)
  - 2b: aerodynamic turn (three-stage: gravity deflection through phi_GA
    on inbound, then lift+drag deflection phi_a inside Mars atmosphere
    with concomitant velocity loss, then post-atmosphere gravity
    deflection through phi_GD)

### 3.2 Method of analysis (p. 369 col 1)

Verbatim: "The round-trip trajectories were studied by first selecting
the type of nonstop trajectory, the launch year, and the total trip time
(equivalent to the central angle from 1 to 3 in Fig 1). Then, by varying
the launch date and the time out, the trajectories with a minimum
propulsive velocity increment were located. The trajectory data were
generated by the 'patch-conic, impulsive Delta-V' computer program of
Ref 1, which accounts for both the eccentricity and the inclinations of
the Earth and the Mars orbits."

**Ref 1**: Knip G. Jr. and Zola C. L., "Three dimensional trajectory
analysis for round-trip missions to Mars", NASA TN D-1316 (1962).

Constraints (verbatim, p. 369 col 1-2):
- Earth parking orbit at 1.1 Earth radii.
- Atmospheric braking at Earth return.
- Lowest Mars periapsis for gravity / propulsive-gravity = 1.1 Mars
  radii.

### 3.3 Quantitative results (Figs 3, 4, p. 369)

Fig 3 (1971 launch year, p. 369 right col):
- Gravity turn possible only at trip times ~1 year AND at trip times
  > 1.5 years (the gap is the central infeasibility region).
- Propulsive-gravity turn: viable over a wide range of trip times.
  Approximately 3 mps minimum DV at ~1 year trip time; "slow increase
  in DV with decreasing trip time for trip times down to about 400
  days" (p. 369 mid col 2).
- Aerodynamic turn with L/D = 4: DV approaches the one-way-transfer
  minimum (~2.2 mps).
- Aerodynamic turn with L/D = 1.5 and L/D = 2.0: "significant
  reductions in DV below that for the propulsive-gravity turn" with
  trip times 380-420 days.

Fig 4 (1980 launch year, p. 369 right col):
- Gravity-turn lowest-DV at trip time 540 days.
- Propulsive-gravity turn: minimum DV slightly less than gravity turn,
  shorter trip time ~500 days.
- Aerodynamic turn DV "least sensitive to the launch year" (p. 369
  col 2).

Reference quantities (annotated on Figs 3-4):
- Total DV for direct lunar landing and return = 5.3 mps
- Minimum DV for one-way Earth-Mars fly-by transfer = ~2.2 mps

### 3.4 Operational notes (p. 369 col 2)

- "g-loads during the atmospheric turns are less than 10 Earth g's,
  which is about the limit of human tolerance"
- "The vehicle must be capable of absorbing both the heat load
  associated with the aerodynamic turn at Mars and the Earth
  atmospheric entry"
- "The lowest DV available will depend on the practically realizable
  lift-drag ratios, which may be of the order of 2.0"
- Concludes that propulsive-gravity and atmospheric-turn trajectories
  "appear to offer the potential of fast, light-weight, nonstop round
  trips to Mars" (p. 370 conclusion 4).

### 3.5 The Mars-eccentricity effect

The 1971 (perihelion-pass) vs 1980 (aphelion-pass) contrast is **the
paper's primary scientific finding** beyond the methodology: gravity and
propulsive-gravity DV are eccentricity-sensitive, but the aerodynamic
turn is not (it adapts to the planet, the planet doesn't constrain it).

## 5. Catalogue impact

**No new catalogue rows directly admitted from this paper.**

Rationale:

- Luidens does not commit to a specific launch epoch / trajectory
  point design with tabulated dates, V_infs, or DSM specifications.
- The Fig 3-4 curves are minimum-DV envelopes parameterised by trip
  time across the launch window; the paper does not select a single
  reference trajectory or report per-flyby states.
- The catalogue requires per-encounter V_inf values for V0 admission
  (per the existing `tito-2018-mars-free-return` schema-v4.7 pattern);
  Luidens does not provide these.

**Cross-reference impact on existing rows:**

The `tito-2018-mars-free-return` row (catalogue v4.7) should gain a
methodology-source / paradigm-predecessor cross-reference to Luidens
1964. Tito 2018's "single Mars gravity assist drops perihelion toward
Venus's orbit" is structurally an INSTANCE of Luidens's "gravity turn"
class (Fig 2a) — the 2018 design adds the modern real-ephemeris solve
(DE421) and the launch-window-specific point design that Luidens did
NOT commit to.

The relationship is the SAME as Niehoff 1970 -> VISIT 1985 (Niehoff
digest, paper 2 this task): paradigm-defining 1960s/1970s paper
followed by a point-design retroactive instance decades later.
