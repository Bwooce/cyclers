## 2. What the paper actually is

This is the foundational systematic numerical survey of the three families of
3D periodic halo orbits about the collinear libration points L1, L2, L3 in the
circular restricted three-body problem (CR3BP), tracked **as a function of the
mass ratio mu from 0 to 1**. It extends the Earth-Moon-specific work of
Breakwell & Brown (1979) to arbitrary mass ratio. The headline findings
(Abstract, p. 53):

- Halo families exist near each collinear libration point and extend from the
  libration point **toward the nearest primary** (the secondary m2).
- They appear to exist for **all** mass ratios mu in (0, 1).
- Most families contain a **range of stable orbits**. The L1 family is the
  exception: its stable range shrinks with increasing mu and **disappears at
  mu = 0.0573** (Abstract; restated p. 71). Near L2 and L3, stable orbits
  exist for all mass ratios investigated.
- Orbits **grow in size with increasing mu**.

It is a families-and-stability paper. It tabulates initial conditions for three
specific mass-ratio cases (Tables I, II, III), and otherwise reports the family
structure via projection figures and stability-index curves.

## 3. Model and method

### 3.1 Model (p. 54-55, §2.1)
Standard CR3BP. Rotating barycentric frame, nondimensional units (angular
velocity, m1-m2 separation, and m1+m2 all set to unity). mu = m2/(m1+m2);
1-mu = m1. Equations of motion Eq. (1) with pseudo-potential
`U = (1/2)(x^2+y^2) + (1-mu)/d1 + mu/d2`,
`d1 = [(x+mu)^2+y^2+z^2]^(1/2)`, `d2 = [(x-1+mu)^2+y^2+z^2]^(1/2)`.
Jacobi constant Eq. (2): `C = 2U - (xdot^2+ydot^2+zdot^2)`.
Collinear point convention (p. 55, Fig. 1): **L1 between the primaries, L2 on
the far side of the smaller mass m2, L3 on the far side of the larger mass m1**
(this is the classic/older convention — note it relative to the project's own
L1/L2 labelling when cross-referencing). For mu > 0.5 the point to the right
(previously L2) is relabelled L3 since now m2 > m1 (p. 67, §3.3).

### 3.2 Method — differential correction on the half-period (p. 56-57, §2.2)
Closely follows Breakwell & Brown (1979). Uses the x-z-plane mirror symmetry
(invariance under y -> -y, t -> -t): a periodic halo is sought as a state that
starts **perpendicular to the x-z plane**
`X0 = (x0, 0, z0, 0, ydot0, 0)^T`
and returns to a second perpendicular crossing at the half-period
`X(T/2) = (x, 0, z, 0, ydot, 0)^T`.

Integration: Kutta-Merson, stepping until y changes sign, then step-halving
until |y| < 1e-11 (that time is T/2). The orbit is accepted as periodic if
|xdot| and |zdot| < 1e-8 at T/2. If not, two of the three free ICs are
corrected via the 6x6 state-transition matrix `Phi(T/2,0)` (Eqs. 3-4 set up the
42-equation variational system; a time regularization dt/dtau = d2 near m2
brings it to 43 equations). Two corrector variants are given: Eq. (5) holds x0
fixed and adjusts (z0, ydot0); Eq. (6) holds z0 fixed and adjusts (x0, ydot0).
Convergence is rapid — **3 to 4 iterations** typical (p. 57).

**This is a numerical continuation + differential-corrector method, NOT an
analytic series.** Howell explicitly contrasts with the analytic Lindstedt-
Poincare third-order series of Farquhar & Kamel (1973), whose convergence
"deteriorates as orbit size increases" (p. 54). The ~1000 orbits computed
(p. 58, §3) were generated by natural-parameter continuation: each new orbit's
initial guess is the converged IC of the previous orbit, the family seeded from
Breakwell & Brown's Earth-Moon (mu~0.012) values.

### 3.3 Stability (p. 57-59)
The monodromy matrix `Phi(T,0)` is reconstructed from `Phi(T/2,0)` via the
symmetry relation Eq. (11) (no second-half integration needed). Two eigenvalues
are always 1; the other four are reciprocal pairs (lambda, 1/lambda). A reduced
4x4 matrix M (Eq. 12, holding C constant) yields these four directly. Two
stability indices are defined:
`nu_i = (1/2)(lambda_i + 1/lambda_i)`, i = 1, 2.
**An orbit is stable iff |nu_1| <= 1 AND |nu_2| <= 1 with nu_i real** (p. 59).

## 4. Mass-ratio coverage and tabulated initial conditions

### 4.1 Which mu values
Examples cited in text (p. 54-55): Earth-Moon (0.012), Sun-Jupiter (9.5e-4),
Sun-Earth (3e-6). The actual computed/plotted survey grid:
- **L1 family**: detailed at mu=0.04 (Table I, figs 2-3,5-6); stability curves
  also at mu = 0.06, 0.05, 0.02, 0.01, and 3e-6 (Sun-Earth) and 0.001
  (Sun-Jupiter) (Figs. 4, 6, p. 62; mu_crit = 0.0573 for stability loss).
- **L2 family**: detailed at mu=0.04 (Table II, figs 7,9); stability/stable-
  region curves also at mu = 0.5, 0.32, 0.20, 0.10 (Figs. 8, 10, pp. 65-66).
- **L3 family**: detailed at mu=0.96 (Table III, figs 11,13); stability curves
  also at mu = 0.50, 0.66, 0.76, 0.88 (Figs. 12, 14, pp. 69-70).

### 4.2 Earth-Moon coverage — IMPORTANT for our use
**The paper does NOT tabulate Earth-Moon (mu~0.0121) halo initial conditions.**
Earth-Moon appears only as:
- a named example mass ratio (p. 54: "0.012"),
- the **seed** of the continuation: the study "was started by using values
  obtained by Breakwell and Brown in the Earth-Moon case (mu ~ 0.012)" (p. 58),
- and a stated piece of **future work**: "More investigations of the L3 family
  may also include mu=0.988, which represents the Earth-Moon case" (p. 71 —
  i.e. L3 of Earth-Moon = Moon-Earth with the heavier body as m2).

So Earth-Moon halo numeric ICs must be pulled from **Breakwell & Brown (1979),
Celest. Mech. 20, 389** (the cited source), NOT from this paper. Howell 1984
gives Earth-Moon only as a taxonomy/structure anchor, not as a coordinate
table.

- L2, mu=0.04: C in [~2.988, ~3.263].
- L3, mu=0.96: C in [~1.12, ~2.44].
These are CR3BP normalized Jacobi values at the **listed mu**, not Earth-Moon.

## 5. Halo family structure (taxonomy)

- **Bifurcation origin**: halos branch from the **planar Lyapunov family** at a
  critical in-plane amplitude. Howell frames this via the linearized motion
  (citing Farquhar/Kamel, p. 54): in-plane periodic motion plus simple-harmonic
  out-of-plane motion; "for certain large values of the in-plane amplitude, a
  corresponding value of the out-of-plane amplitude would produce a purely
  periodic three-dimensional path." That amplitude-matched 3D orbit is the
  halo. (The paper does not itself trace the planar-Lyapunov-to-halo
  bifurcation point numerically — it picks up the family already 3D, seeded
  from Breakwell & Brown.)
- **Single-lobe out-of-plane (k_z > 0)**: each halo is a single closed loop
  encircling the libration point with non-zero out-of-plane amplitude z (Figs.
  2, 7, 11). It is symmetric about the x-z plane (mirror symmetry is the basis
  of the half-period corrector).
- **Northern / southern symmetry**: the computed family is "northern"
  (z0 > 0); the "southern" mirror image follows by z -> -z (p. 60). This is the
  exact (northern, southern) halo pair degeneracy our discovery sweep must
  collapse when matching.
- **Family extent**: each family is a one-parameter continuation from near the
  libration point (small amplitude, planar-Lyapunov-like) toward the nearest
  primary m2 (large amplitude, near-rectilinear). As orbits approach m2 they
  grow in out-of-plane amplitude (z_max up), and the period **decreases** for
  L1/L2 (near-collision almost-rectilinear orbits at the m2 end). For L3 the
  orbits become very long and thin near m2 with only slightly decreasing period
  (p. 68).
- **mu-dependence**: increasing mu moves the L1 stable zone toward the
  libration point and enlarges orbits; for L2/L3 the stable zone moves toward
  m2 and enlarges with mu (out-of-plane amplitude grows). At mu=0.05 the L2
  z_max values are ~4x those at mu=0.04 (p. 67).

This is the classic vertical-bifurcation halo topology our #434/#438 sweep
needs: **k_z>0 single-loop orbits about a collinear libration point, born from
the planar Lyapunov family, occurring in northern/southern mirror pairs.**

## 6. Stability findings (summary)

- **L1**: a "middle" stable zone exists at low mu (mu=0.04 stable band
  x0 in [0.729988, 0.801125], Table I / Fig. 3). The stable range shrinks as mu
  grows and **vanishes at mu ~ 0.0573** (nu_1 fails to dip below +1) — the one
  collinear point with no stable halos at high mass ratio (p. 62, p. 71).
- **L2**: stable halos persist for all mu tested. A "middle" stable zone roughly
  halfway between m2 and L2; for mu=0.5 a second near-m2 stable zone of
  near-collision orbits appears, and for mu > 0.5 the two stable regions may
  merge (p. 65-66).
- **L3** (mu > 0.5 continuation of L2): stable zone persists and merges with the
  near-collision stable zone; mu=0.80 is the highest mu at which two separate
  stable regions are distinguishable; numerical difficulties (long thin
  near-collision orbits) cap the survey near m2 at high mu (p. 69-70).

## 7. For our use (#434/#438 known_corpus_3d)

### 7.1 What this paper gives us
- **A taxonomy anchor, NOT Earth-Moon coordinate ICs.** This is the explicit
  answer to the task's central question: Howell 1984 provides the canonical
  *structure* of the L1/L2/L3 halo families across mu, but it does **not**
  tabulate Earth-Moon (mu~0.0121) halo ICs. Earth-Moon halos must be sourced
  from **Breakwell & Brown 1979 (Celest. Mech. 20, 389)** — the seed reference
  Howell continued from. (Useful follow-up acquisition target if not already in
  corpus.)
- **Three concrete CR3BP coordinate tables** at mu = 0.04 (L1, L2) and mu = 0.96
  (L3), each a six-member `(x0, z0, ydot0, T/2, C, nu_1, nu_2)` set. These are
  golden anchors for validating any halo continuation engine the project builds
  — same model (CR3BP), independently published, exact numbers. They are the
  right same-model golden target for an L1/L2 halo corrector test (cf. the
  orbit-closure-discipline memory's "same-model golden target" rule).
- **The structural fingerprint** our sweep needs to flag a candidate as a KNOWN
  halo: (a) single-loop orbit about a collinear libration point, (b) z_max > 0
  out-of-plane amplitude, (c) x-z-plane mirror symmetry, (d) perpendicular
  x-z-plane crossings at t=0 and T/2 with ICs of the form
  `(x0, 0, z0, 0, ydot0, 0)`, (e) occurring in northern/southern (z -> -z)
  pairs, (f) on a one-parameter family from small-amplitude (planar-Lyapunov
  bifurcation) to large-amplitude near-rectilinear (near m2). A swept orbit with
  this signature at any collinear point is a halo and must be returned KNOWN,
  not novel.

### 7.2 Vertical-Lyapunov vs halo distinction
The task references "L1/L2 vertical-Lyapunov + halo-class members." Note: this
paper covers **halos only** — the planar-Lyapunov family is mentioned only as
the bifurcation parent, and the **vertical (axial) Lyapunov** family is NOT
treated here at all. So Howell 1984 anchors the halo class but does NOT anchor
the vertical-Lyapunov class; that needs a separate reference (e.g. a
Doedel/Gomez/Calleja-type CR3BP catalogue, or Restrepo-Russell 2018 which is
planar-only and therefore also does not cover it). Worth a corpus gap note.
