# ML-surrogate investigation — surrogate-pruned scan + basin classifier

**Date:** 2026-06-07
**Scope:** docs-only paper investigation. No source/test/data changed. Evaluates
whether the ML-surrogate *pattern* from three low-thrust papers could accelerate
OUR impulsive/ballistic (Lambert + corrector) search, ending in a concrete
BUILD / DON'T-BUILD recommendation for two candidate builds.
**Context read first:** `docs/notes/2026-06-06-performance-profile.md`,
`docs/notes/2026-06-07-mbh-wrapper.md`,
`docs/notes/2026-06-07-external-algorithms-survey.md`.

## Framing (read first)

Our search is IMPULSIVE/ballistic (Lambert legs + scipy correctors), NOT
low-thrust. All three papers learn *low-thrust* cost/feasibility, so the trained
models do not transfer — the question is whether the surrogate **pattern**
(learn a cheap predictor of the expensive solve; use it to prune/propose) is
worth building for us. The papers' own numbers settle it, because they tell us
(a) how many exact solves a useful surrogate needs and (b) what the surrogate
actually replaces. Our exact solves are cheap by their standards
(circular ballistic = 31.6 ms, astropy ballistic = 360.9 ms,
`ballistic_correct` S1L1 cell; maintenance Aldrin = 16.75 s astropy — all from
the performance profile), which inverts their economics.

**Evidence-culture constraint (states up front, governs both verdicts):** a
surrogate may PRUNE and PROPOSE but may never constitute EVIDENCE. Every
survivor must still pass the exact solver + the sourced-anchor gates
(the campaign's `CLOSE-AND-MATCH` test against published V∞ / period). This is
not optional polish — it is the same rule that makes Paper 3's pipeline sound
(it re-solves every surviving leg with a full OCP, §IV step 3, p. 17).

## Paper 1 — Zhang, Acciarini, Izzo, Baoyin, Topputo, *Pretrained Approximators for Low-Thrust Trajectory Cost and Reachability*, arXiv:2605.26790v2 (27 May 2026)

**1. Architecture + I/O.** Two **Value Networks** (regressors), one for
fuel-optimal Δv_min and one for time-optimal Δt_min. The Δt network doubles as
the **reachability** predictor: "a transfer is unreachable if the specified
transfer time is shorter than the minimum, and reachable only once this lower
bound is exceeded… Framing reachability prediction as a time-optimal control
problem thus provides a continuous and physically meaningful measure, offering
richer information than a simple binary classification" (p. 3). It learns a
*scalar cost*, not a feasibility class. Map (Fig. 7, p. 15):
`f(r1,v1,r2,v2,Δt,m0,Tmax,Isp,μ) → Δv_min or Δt_min`. Single-rev = 9-layer
×128-neuron MLP; multi-rev = residual MLP with skip connections, 9×128, Softplus
(p. 19, Table 9). Decisive detail for us: **the best-accuracy input is itself a
Lambert solution** — "Lambert_sph" (`e, f, Δv1, Δv2` in spherical coords) is the
top input config (Table 2/3, p. 14), i.e. the network is a *correction* on top
of a Lambert solve, and the model is deliberately sized so "inference time
remains on the same order of magnitude as that of a Lambert solver" (p. 8).

**2. Training-data generation (THE CRUX).** Dataset = "up to approximately 100
million samples" (p. 22), scaling-law study spanning N_data = 10^5…10^8 (Fig. 2,
p. 8). Each sample is an indirect-method (Pontryagin TPBVP, shooting) low-thrust
solve, generated by the "Homotopy Ray Method" (Algorithm 1, p. 9) which reuses
the previous costate as the next initial guess to make the shooting converge
cheaply. Generation ran "in C++ on a workstation with an AMD EPYC 7452
(2.6 GHz, 64 cores) and 256 GB RAM" (p. 15); the sibling paper (Paper 2) puts a
number on it: "approximately three days to produce each 1-million-sample block"
(Paper 2, p. 8). So the published model cost **~300 core-days** of solve time to
label.

**3. Speedup vs accuracy loss / false-negatives.** Δv MAE 3.38 m/s (single-rev,
0.014% MRE) / 99.40 m/s (multi-rev, 0.003% MRE); Δt MAE 2.56 d / 0.63%
(Table 10/11, pp. 22–23). Porkchop generation "in negligible time" vs
"high-fidelity optimal-control solvers typically require days to generate a
single porkchop plot" (p. 25). It is a *regressor*, so "false-negative" is not
reported as a class rate; the relevant risk is that the worst-1000 cases cluster
exactly where we'd care — "more challenging transfers, characterized by higher
fuel consumption, longer time-of-flight, and larger Lambert cost, tend to appear
in the worst-performing cases" (p. 20), and accuracy degrades with revolution
count (top-1000 = 742 zero-rev; worst-1000 = 169 three-rev, p. 21). Our cyclers
are long multi-rev arcs → the regime where this surrogate is *least* accurate.

**4. Generalisation.** The headline contribution: a **self-similar transform**
(rotational + dimensional invariance, §4.1, pp. 11–13) maps families that differ
only by scaling/orientation to one representative case, so "the same neural
approximator can be applied to diverse orbital environments and mission classes"
across semi-major axis, inclination, and central bodies **without retraining**
(abstract; p. 2). This is the strongest generalisation claim of the three — but
it is a claim about *low-thrust scalar cost*, not about our basin topology.

## Paper 2 — Zhang, Michelotti, Pinho, Zou, Topputo, *Optimal Control and Neural Porkchop Analysis for Low-Thrust Asteroid Rendezvous*, Astronautics 2026, 1(1):6 (MDPI)

**1. Architecture + I/O.** Same lineage — "a pretrained model is adopted
directly" from Paper 1's reference [41] (p. 8). Two MLPs, 9 layers ×128 neurons,
ReLU, predicting Δv (fuel) and Δt (time/reachability). Reachability rule
identical: "if the given transfer time is shorter than the predicted minimum
transfer time, the transfer is considered infeasible" (p. 8).

**2. Training-data generation.** "Training was performed on a 100-million-sample
dataset, which requires approximately three days to produce each 1-million-sample
block, culminating in a total generation time of three days for the full dataset"
(p. 8) — i.e. heavily parallel; per-block cost confirms ~3 days/1M on the same
EPYC platform.

**3. Speedup vs accuracy / failure mode (most useful finding here).** Δv MAE
3.38 m/s / 0.78% MRE; Δt MAE 2.56 d / 0.63% (p. 9). BUT the paper's whole point
is a **documented surrogate failure under realistic constraints**: with path
constraints (0.8 AU floor, engine off > 1.15 AU) the surrogate diverges from the
constrained truth — Fig. 11 (p. 14) shows a cell where numerical m_fuel = 2.6 kg
but the surrogate predicts 1.2 kg, **relative error 55%**, and the surrogate's
unconstrained trajectory violates the 0.8 AU floor while the true one does not.
"the current neural network model may produce large estimation errors when
physical path constraints are present" (p. 13). Translation to our case: the
surrogate is **optimistic** where a hard constraint bites — it will say "feasible
/ cheap" exactly where the real problem is infeasible. For a *completeness*
search that is the safe direction (over-propose, never silently discard); for a
*cost* claim it is poison.

**4. Generalisation.** Same self-similar dataset → "strong generalization across
arbitrary orbital parameters (a, e, i) and propulsion configurations… without
requiring dataset retraining" (p. 9). Multi-rev is NOT native — it needs a
hybrid PSO + gradient wrapper with per-segment feasibility penalties (§2.3.3,
pp. 9–10).

## Paper 3 — Viavattene & Ceriotti, *Artificial Neural Networks for Multiple NEA Rendezvous Missions with Continuous Thrust*, J. Spacecraft & Rockets 59(2):574-586 (2021), DOI 10.2514/1.A34799

This is the one whose *architecture* matches Build A (prune-then-verify), so it
gets the closest read.

**1. Architecture + I/O.** Feedforward MLP, **4 hidden layers ×80 neurons**,
sigmoid, Levenberg–Marquardt (Table 4, p. 11). Input = MEE of departure +
arrival orbits + phasing (MEE chosen, correlation 0.925, Table 3, p. 10);
output = `[ΔV, TOF]` of the transfer (a 2-vector regressor). The ANN output
**feeds a tree-search** that, per leg, keeps only `N_max = 200` best transfers
(by TOF) and expands those (Fig. 5/6, pp. 13–14). **Every surviving sequence is
then re-solved leg-by-leg by a full OCP** (Radau pseudospectral + IPOPT NLP) and
"built if at least one feasible solution is found for each leg" (§IV step 3,
p. 17). The ANN prunes; the optimiser is the evidence.

**2. Training-data generation.** Database = **10,100 transfers** (permutations of
100 NEAs, p. 8) — note three to four orders of magnitude SMALLER than Papers 1/2.
Critically, the labels were NOT generated by full optimal control: "a shape-based
approach is used in this study to generate the training database… there is no
optimization involved, so there is no guarantee that the solution found is
optimal. However, the shape of the minimum-cost rendezvous trajectory can be
approximated… Thus, this choice is appropriate for the preliminary search"
(p. 8). The shape-based label costs ~48 s each (p. 24), so the whole database
≈ 10,100 × 48 s ≈ **5.6 core-days**.

**3. Speedup vs accuracy / false-negatives.** Speed (Table 11, p. 26): one
transfer estimate **0.49 s (ANN) vs 48 s (shape-based)**; a full sequence-search
run **7.66 hours (ANN) vs 33 days** → "almost 100 times faster." Accuracy
(Table 10, p. 23): mean error 7.8% TOF, 9.9% ΔV for SEP (13.4% for the
out-of-training solar-sail case). The pruner keeps the 200 best **by TOF** and
relies on the downstream OCP to reject bad survivors — so a ~10% ΔV error in the
*pruner* is tolerated precisely because nothing the ANN says is final. There is
no claim of zero false-negatives; the design assumes the ranker is noisy and
buys completeness back by keeping a wide N_max and re-verifying.

**4. Generalisation.** Trained on 100 NEAs, applied to the full 6286-object /
2768-object catalogue at launch dates *outside* the training window to "test the
generalization property" (p. 13); generalises across orbits and epochs within
the near-Earth low-thrust regime. Retrained per propulsion regime (SEP vs solar
sail degrades to 13.4%).

## Synthesis — the two candidate builds

### Build A — Surrogate-pruned scan (a cheap filter in front of the Lambert/corrector grid)

The proposal: train a regressor/classifier to predict "is this grid cell worth
an exact solve?" and skip the rest, replacing brute force over the 2816 pts/row
grid (`search/scan.py`).

**The bar it must beat (the dumb-but-sound alternatives we already have):**
`#128-S2` ephemeris memoisation + vectorisation. From the performance profile:
42% of per-solve `state()` calls are exact (body,epoch) duplicates → exact-memo
gives **−37% wall**; epoch-grid vectorisation gives a **−70% ceiling**
(360 → ~100 ms/solve), both with **ZERO false-negative risk** (results are
bit-identical or within float tolerance — they are below the residual layer,
survive #122, and require no labels).

**Break-even arithmetic (shown).** The papers tell us a useful surrogate's data
cost. Take the most favourable analogue — Paper 3, the smallest dataset and the
one that learns ΔV+TOF for a prune-and-verify pipeline: **~10,100 labelled
solves** to train an adequately-accurate (≈10% ΔV) ranker. Our exact labels are
the *correctors themselves*:

- At the circular ballistic cost (31.6 ms/solve): 10,100 × 0.0316 s ≈ **319 s**
  to generate the corpus — trivial. BUT a surrogate that predicts the *circular*
  solve saves us ~32 ms/cell while costing a forward-pass; Paper 1 deliberately
  sizes the net so inference ≈ a Lambert solve, and our circular cell is already
  *Lambert-bound, not ephemeris-bound* (profile Target 1: circular state ≈
  0.5 ms/solve; the 31.6 ms is Lambert + scipy finite-diff jacobian). A net
  whose inference costs ~one Lambert call cannot meaningfully undercut a solve
  that is one-to-a-few Lambert calls. **No net speedup at the circular tier.**
- At the astropy ballistic cost (360.9 ms/solve), where there IS fat to cut:
  10,100 × 0.361 s ≈ **3,650 s ≈ 1 core-hour** to label — also affordable. The
  catch is accuracy. Paper 1's own finding is that error is WORST for the
  high-rev, high-cost regime (p. 20–21) — which is exactly our long multi-rev
  cycler arcs — and Paper 2 shows 55% error the moment a hard constraint bites
  (p. 14). A pruner that is least reliable precisely on our hardest, most
  interesting cells either (a) prunes true solutions (forbidden — "no silent
  caps") or (b) is set conservative enough that it prunes almost nothing.

The deciding comparison: **#128-S2 delivers −37% (guaranteed) to −70% (ceiling)
of scan wall, today, with no labels and no false-negative risk.** A surrogate
pruner would have to beat −70% AND carry a false-negative budget of zero to be
worth it. On the astropy tier the surrogate's *inference* (sized at ~1 Lambert
call) plus the unavoidable exact re-solve of every survivor (evidence rule) means
its best case is "skip the exact solve on cells it confidently rejects" — but the
profile says the duplicate-call memo already removes the redundant ephemeris work
those cells share, and vectorisation removes the framework overhead globally,
both without ever risking a miss. The surrogate adds a labels-pipeline, a
training/validation burden, a model-drift maintenance tail, and a
false-negative-tuning problem to chase a slice that −70% memoisation already
mostly captures.

**Verdict A — DON'T BUILD.** #128-S2 memoisation + vectorisation dominates it on
every axis that matters: equal-or-better wall reduction, zero false-negative
risk, no training corpus, below-residual survivability. The surrogate's
accuracy is worst exactly in our multi-rev/constrained regime (Paper 1 p. 20–21,
Paper 2 p. 14), so it cannot be trusted as a pruner without a conservative
threshold that prunes almost nothing — at which point it is pure overhead. Build
A only becomes interesting in a hypothetical future where (i) #128-S2 is already
shipped, (ii) the per-cell exact solve is many *seconds* (not the 0.36 s astropy
ballistic cell — only the 16.75 s *maintenance* solve qualifies, and that is not
a grid scan), and (iii) we have a persisted labelled corpus. None of these hold
now.

### Build B — Basin classifier (predict in-family vs degenerate basin from seed features before a 12–19 s solve)

The proposal: before paying a 12–19 s astropy closed solve, predict from seed
features whether the seed lands in the sourced (in-family, low-V∞) basin or the
degenerate high-V∞ basin (the #110/#120/#122 disease). False negatives are
TOLERABLE here — an MBH hop recovers from a mis-routed seed
(`docs/notes/2026-06-07-mbh-wrapper.md`, Gate 2), unlike Build A.

This is the better-motivated build: the economics finally favour a surrogate
(the solve being skipped is 12–19 s, ~40–600× the astropy ballistic cell), and
the false-negative tolerance removes the completeness objection. The papers'
pattern (Paper 3's prune-then-verify with a noisy ranker + wide keep) fits, and
classification is *easier* than Paper 1/2's regression.

**The blocker is data, and it is decisive.** Build B's stated advantage is that
"we already generate labelled (seed → basin) pairs as a by-product of every
campaign." I checked. `scripts/campaign_russell12.py` **does** classify every
row outcome into `CLOSE-AND-MATCH` / `CLOSE-OFF-ANCHOR` / `NO-CLOSE` with
achieved-vs-sourced V∞ per encounter (lines 230–299, 484–505) — these ARE
labelled (seed → basin) pairs in the right shape. But:

- **They are not persisted.** A repo-wide search found **zero** saved campaign
  result artefacts (no `*.json`/`*.csv` under any `*russell*`/`*campaign*`
  path); results are printed to stdout / an optional `--out` file and discarded.
  The corpus is regenerated and thrown away each run.
- **The universe is tiny.** The campaign covers the **12** Russell rows; the
  catalogue has ~812 russell-tagged rows of ~1123 total, but the vast majority
  are gapped (no sourced anchors) so cannot be *labelled* `CLOSE-AND-MATCH` at
  all. Even labelling every anchored row gives O(10^2) examples, and each is one
  (row, seed) point, not the thousands of seed perturbations a classifier needs
  to learn a basin *boundary*.

Compare to Paper 3's **10,100** examples for a 2-output regressor over a far
smoother low-thrust cost surface. Our basin boundary is sharper (the #137
finding: a single ellipse's aphelion+transit and its V∞ describe *different*
topologies — the boundary is not a smooth function of the obvious descriptors),
so we would likely need MORE data than Paper 3, not less, to learn it reliably.

**Break-even arithmetic (shown).** To build a useful corpus we must generate
labelled seeds deliberately. At the maintenance-class solve cost (16.75 s
astropy) a Paper-3-scale 10,100-example corpus costs
10,100 × 16.75 s ≈ **47 core-hours ≈ 2 core-days**; at the cheaper astropy
ballistic cell (0.361 s) it is ~1 core-hour but those cells are not the 12–19 s
solves we want to *skip*, so they mis-label the target distribution. 2 core-days
is affordable in absolute terms — the real cost is the labelling+training+
validation+drift pipeline and a sourced-anchor harness to keep the labels honest,
all to skip a solve that **MBH already makes robust to mis-seeding** for free
(`mbh.py` Gate 2: a 40-day mis-seed that the plain solve cannot recover is
recovered by 60 MBH hops). The classifier would *route seeds faster*, but MBH
*already cures the routing failure* the classifier is meant to pre-empt, and MBH
needs no corpus.

**Verdict B — BUILD AFTER persisting a labelled corpus AND only if MBH proves
insufficient.** Two preconditions, in order:
1. **First (cheap, do anyway):** make the campaign **persist** its per-seed
   outcome labels (the `CLOSE-AND-MATCH`/`CLOSE-OFF-ANCHOR` + achieved/sourced
   V∞ dicts it already computes) to a versioned `*.json`/`*.csv`, and have the
   MBH wrapper log every (seed → accepted-basin) hop. This is a few lines, has
   standalone value (audit trail, regression baselines), and is the *only* way a
   corpus ever accumulates. Until this exists, Build B is unbuildable — there is
   no training data on disk.
2. **Then, gated on evidence:** once O(10^3–10^4) persisted labelled seeds exist
   AND MBH's basin-selection (which is the cheaper, no-corpus cure already shown
   working) is demonstrably *insufficient* on real-ephemeris rows, train a
   classifier as a *seed router* in front of MBH — never as a gate. False
   negatives stay tolerable only because every survivor still hits the exact
   solver + sourced-anchor check. If MBH + the future sub-basin selector
   (mbh-wrapper note, Adoption §) closes the rows, Build B is unnecessary.

**Do NOT build the classifier now:** the data does not exist on disk, the
anchored universe is O(10^2) not O(10^4), and MBH is a cheaper cure for the same
disease that needs no labels.

## The single most surprising thing in the papers

Paper 1's best-performing surrogate input is *itself a Lambert solution*
("Lambert_sph" wins Tables 2 and 3, p. 14), and the network is deliberately
sized so its inference costs about the same as one Lambert solve (p. 8). The
"neural surrogate" is therefore a thin learned **correction on top of a Lambert
call** — it does not replace the cheap classical solve, it *consumes* it. For a
codebase whose inner loop already IS Lambert (and whose ballistic cells are
Lambert-bound, not the thing the surrogate accelerates), this collapses the
apparent speedup: you cannot beat "one Lambert call" with "a Lambert call plus a
neural net." The papers' 100×–1000× speedups are all measured against
*low-thrust optimal-control* solves (days per porkchop), a baseline we do not
have. Against our actual baseline the surrogate's own design assumptions erase
its advantage.

## Bottom line

- **Build A (surrogate-pruned scan): DON'T BUILD.** Dominated by #128-S2
  memoisation/vectorisation (−37% guaranteed, −70% ceiling, zero false-negative
  risk, no corpus). Surrogate accuracy is worst exactly in our multi-rev regime
  (Paper 1 p. 20–21) and optimistic where constraints bite (Paper 2 p. 14, 55%
  error) — unusable as a completeness-preserving pruner.
- **Build B (basin classifier): BUILD AFTER persisting a labelled corpus, and
  only if MBH proves insufficient.** The economics and false-negative tolerance
  finally favour a surrogate, but the claimed by-product corpus is **not on disk**
  (zero saved artefacts; campaign covers only 12 rows), and MBH already cures the
  same basin-selection failure with no labels. Cheap precondition: make the
  campaign + MBH **persist** their already-computed (seed → basin) labels.
- **Governing rule for either:** surrogates may prune and propose, never
  constitute evidence — every survivor passes the exact solver + sourced-anchor
  gate. This is exactly Paper 3's sound design (ANN ranks; full OCP decides).
